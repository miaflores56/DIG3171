Ageism and Sexism in a World of Production. When observing a job market, one may not realize how age and gender can affect a person’s chance of succeeding in it or let alone enter the market. With this project, I aim to dive into the concept of how age or gender may be able to hinder how a media production job market may perceive them. I aim to explain how age and gender are connected, whether directly or indirectly, to a person’s success in the job market. I want to explore this project from the perspective of the person hiring a candidate. With this idea in mind, I wanted to create something that would take the reader by surprise. I used Tracery to aid me in creating a code that produces randomly generated “candidates” to make it feel as if the reader is the hiring manager of a big company, and their job is to choose a candidate. The candidates consist of randomized names, positions, ages, and experience. With this code, I want to dive into the candidates people would choose and examine the reasons why. In recent studies, it is found that algorithms “[Assume] that women are younger and less experienced, rating older male applicants as of higher quality.” (Guilbeault et al.). With studies like these, it is often found that when thinking of men and women in a workplace, people often assume the woman is younger and therefore less experienced. (Guilbeault et al 3). When notions like these are reaffirmed, often in the media, it is more difficult for the victims of said notions to succeed in the workplace. With the help of Tracery in this project, I hope to shed light on the idea that age and gender play more of a role in the success of a candidate in the workplace than most people realize. Guilbeault, D., Delecourt, S., & Desikan, B. S. (2025). Age and gender distortion in online media and large language models. Nature. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09581-z
